Hat League Ratings

BUDA Rating and Team Performance¶

I'm excited to announce that I have been given access to the BUDA database of player ratings! This means that I can compare my system of rating players with the one that is currently in use by BUDA.

Once again, let's dive straight in with the data: the relationship between BUDA Rating and team performance (represented by average plus/minus per game). Each dot on this plot is a spring hat league team in the "JP Mixed (4/3)" division. I've included teams from 2010 through the present. The BUDA Rating shown is the average of the BUDA ratings for the players on that team.

Central conclusion: there is no obvious correlation between team performance and BUDA Rating. The Pearson correlation coefficient is a paltry 0.095, and the p-value of 0.21 indicates that there is a 1-in-5 chance that this distribution of data could have been drawn randomly. In other words, you can't use BUDA Rating to predict team performance. This is a sensible result, since hat league is designed to assign players to teams in a way that maximizes parity league-wide, and BUDA Rating is the metric by which BUDA has assigned players to teams.

Club Rating and Team Performance¶

Now let's take a look at the relationship between team performance and the Club Rating that I've developed over the past few months. As a brief refresher, it's based on the idea that most players participate in club leagues in which there is a vetting process that determines whether or not a player can join a team. This vetting process can be as simple as a recommendation from a friend already on the team to something as complicated as a full tryout. So if someone plays on a high-level club team, they will have a high Club Rating. If they play on a low-level club team, they will have a low Club Rating.

Central conclusion: Club Rating correlates strongly with team performance! The Pearson correlation coefficient is now 0.43, and the p-value is 3.9e-9--there is no chance this distribution of points was obtained randomly. This means that Club Rating can be used to predict team performance.

Note: I've converted Club Rating to the same scale as BUDA Rating for convenience of comparison.

Note: Keen eyes may notice that the outlier from spring hat league 2016 is no longer as obvious in this diagram. This is because I'm now using information from the BUDA player rating database to fill in when club playing experience data is not available.

We can dig a bit further to understand why BUDA Rating is less predictive of team performance than Club Rating. Each player's BUDA Rating is the arithmetic mean of their Self Rating and their Captain Rating. Self Rating is the rating that player has provided about themselves. I.e., they indicate how far they can throw the disc, how fast they run, etc. Captain Rating is the rating that one or more captains have provided about that player (based on answers to the same questions used to calculate Self Rating).

Captains are volunteers -- there is no requirement to provide ratings for their players, and often they don't. In fact, about 55% of players have no Captain Rating. In this situation, BUDA Rating is equal to Self Rating.

Note: a player's Captain Rating is used in place of Club Rating if experience at the club level is not available. If no captains have rated a given player, then Self Rating is used in place of Captain Rating. If no captains have rated a given player AND that player has no club experience, then Self Rating is used in place of Club Rating.

Self Rating and Team Performance¶

Let's compare Self Rating to team performance.

There is a striking, negative correlation between Self Rating and team performance! In other words, the higher a team's average Self Rating, the more they are outscored by their opponents. How can this be? There are two plausible explanations, both of which are likely at work.

  1. First, experienced players are aware that if they "sandbag" -- i.e., rate themselves lower than they actually are -- then they are likely to have stronger teammates and therefore likely to win more games. There are many examples in the database of this effect, some more extreme than others.

  2. Second, newer players lack the experience needed to rate themselves accurately. They may under-rate or over-rate their skill level, simply because they are unfamiliar with the average league-wide skill level. In a league with 24 teams (as is the case for spring hat league), there will be teams that randomly happen to have a cluster of players who over-rated themselves. These teams will have a tough road ahead of them.

Captain Rating and Team Performance¶

Finally, let's compare Captain Rating to team performance.

Captain Rating correlates strongly with team performance, although not as strongly as Club Rating (Pearson correlation coefficient of 0.31 vs. 0.43). It is clear that Captain Rating is far more predictive of true skill level than Self Rating. This makes sense simply because captains have much more information at their disposal. For example, they can rate the players on their team in a relative sense ("I need to make sure that Player X's rating is higher than Player Y's"). In fact, it might be the case that Captain Rating would outperform Club Rating if more than 45% of players had captain's ratings. Given that captains are volunteers and busy people (like everyone else), it seems unreasonable to expect this percentage to change significantly in the future.

Recommendations¶

Going forward, I recommend that BUDA use Club Rating and Captain Rating to determine player ratings in future hat leagues. Self Rating should be avoided whenever possible for player ratings.

Next steps¶

  • Identify optimal combination of Club Rating and Captain Rating that yields New BUDA Rating.
  • Work with BUDA admins to implement New BUDA Rating for upcoming Holiday Hat League 2016 (starts in late October).
  • Investigate forecasting team performance not just by average Club Rating for players on the team, but also by average Club Rating for each quartile (i.e., average Club Rating for top 25% of players on the team, second-best 25%, third-best 25%, and bottom 25%). The idea is that the top 25% of players on a team might be more impactful than the bottom 25%. This method would be able to take this effect into account.
